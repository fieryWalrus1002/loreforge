Mutant Animals
Most mutated fauna are merely deformed or sickly ver-
sions of ordinary animal life. Some creatures, however,
are cursed with exceptional and dangerous qualities by
the strange energies that changed them.
In the example setting of the Albuquerque Death
Zone, many of these changes are the fruit of the mal-
functioning Highshine civil defense system. The
ambient nanites that were once meant to contain di-
sasters and preserve human life were perverted by a
maddened psychic, and now act to change victims in
new and often terrible ways.
Adjusting Animal Abilities
The mutant animals listed here are only the more aver-
age examples of their kind. Particularly strong or fee-
ble creatures might have more or fewer hit dice, from
twice as many to half as many, rounded up.
Each additional hit die adds 1d8 hit points and a
+1 bonus to the creature’s hit bonus, while subtracting
hit dice lowers their HP and gives them a -1 to hit for
each one lost.
Blinder Bird
A Highshined owl derivative with a long, serrated beak
and glass-sharp claws, a blinder bird has a special crav-
ing for blood and a tendency to aim for a victim’s eyes.
The bird swoops down silently to rake its unsuspecting
prey before licking the blood or aqueous humour from
its claws.
Blinder birds are usually found in flocks of 1d8.
Silent Strike: When encountered, the PC with the best
Wis/Notice in the party must succeed in an op-
posed skill check against the blinder bird’s flock
leader. On a failure, the party is automatically
surprised.
Flaysnake
This species of mutated, man-sized rattlesnake was
cursed by the Highshine. Its altered genome prevents
it from forming scales, leaving its body raw and red
while the Highshine nanites force homeostasis and
prevent it from dying of infection or fluid loss. The
perpetual torment it experiences leaves it with a sav-
age temper.
Flaysnakes are normally found alone or in brief-
ly-mated pairs.
Flaysnake Poison: If the snake bites a victim, the sub-
ject must immediately make a Physical saving
throw. On a success, they resist the poison. On
a failure, they gain 1d6 System Strain and must
make another Physical save next round, continu-
ing until they either succeed or die when their
accumulated System Strain exceeds their Con-
stitution.
Ghoul Bear
A loathsome cross between a black bear’s body and the
fanged head of a human, ghoul bears possess a human
degree of intelligence but never communicate except
to perfectly mimic the cries and pleas of their past vic-
tims. They eat carrion, but are notorious for prowling
around the edges of enclaves, tempting in prey with
stupefying cries for help.
Ghoul bears usually hunt alone, but occasionally
a family of 1d4 of them is encountered.
Voice of the Grave: A ghoul bear’s voice is tuned with
Highshine-laced subsonics. Each round, anyone
within ten meters who hears it must make a Men-
tal save; on a failure, they lose their next round’s
Main Action. A victim will not notice this stupe-
faction unless it’s pointed out by others.
Glow Turtle
These bad-tempered tortoises are as tall as a man at the
peak of their glowing shells and are found anywhere
they can get adequate vegetation to eat. Their bodies
process the accumulated radioactivity and toxins into
their shells, which glow bright blue in warning. Glow
turtles are herbivores but will attack interlopers, and
can move with surprising quickness over short dis-
tances.
Glow turtles are only ever encountered singly.
Glowing Shell: Anyone who gets within 30 meters of
the glow turtle must immediately make a radia-
tion saving throw as per the rules on page 55.
This check is repeated at one-round intervals.
The shell retains its radioactive qualities for 1d6
weeks after the turtle’s death.
Gorehound
These heirs of once-docile domestic pets have been
warped into something more survivable by the High-
shine. Now standing waist-high on a human, their
large braincases betray the augmented intelligence
they display. Some of them are as intelligent as a hu-
man, though all of them are suffused with a malicious,
hateful spirit of sadistic aggression towards non-ca-
nines. They hunt with expert coordination and can
fully understand human tricks and technology.
Gorehounds are found in packs of 2d4 animals.
Occasionally a very intelligent one bonds with a hu-
man of similar cruel temperament, enjoying the left-
overs of their “master” until it becomes convenient to
eat him, too.
Savage Pack: Each gorehound counts as two allies for
all Swarm Attack support. They can easily track
prey by scent, and will chase a victim for days if
it piques their urge for cruelty.

Gut Weasel
These miniature mutated wolverines possess a vicious
disposition, even though they seldom weigh more
than five or six kilos. Their blinding speed and dia-
mond-hard claws aid their preferred hunting tactic of
aiming directly for the entrails of their prey.
Gut weasels are usually solitary, though a mated
pair can sometimes be unfortunately encountered.
Rabid Speed: Gut weasels always win initiative in
combat. Against Alert foes or others with auto-
matic initiative wins, the dice are rolled normally.
Heritor Bug
Foot-long mutant cockroaches, these bugs are individ-
ually feeble but are invariably found in vast swarms.
Their acidic mandibles often leave terrible scars on
those who survive their attacks.
Heritor bug nests usually have 1d20x20 of the in-
sects in the general vicinity.
Ravenous Tide: Anyone brought to zero hit points by
a heritor bug automatically suffers a Major Injury
from the relentless gnawing, even if those rules
aren’t usually used in the campaign.
Manscorpion
These horse-sized devils have the body of a scorpion
mated to a humanoid torso and an insectile, mandibled
head. They prefer to nest in human ruins and use their
hardened climbing claws to ascend to high lairs. They
do not appear to have any human kind of intelligence,
and do not use tools in their hunts. They often ambush
their prey, stinging them and then retreating until
their poison has had time to work.
Manscorpions hunt alone, but their nests will
have 2d6 adults.
Paralytic Poison: One of a manscorpion’s three attacks
will be a sting; a victim struck by this must make
a Physical saving throw immediately and another
at the end of their next round. If they fail both,
they are paralyzed for 1d10+10 minutes.
Wallcrawler: They can traverse walls and ceilings as
if they were flat ground.
Redhorn
These mutant cattle are a scourge on the dry pastures
of the Albuquerque Death Zone. They are omnivo-
rous with a preference for meat, impossible to domes-
ticate, and a constant threat to the herds of the settled
communities. They appear as emaciated bovines with
curved horns, hidden fangs, and retractable hoof-
claws. Some are clever enough to hide among herds of
more ordinary cattle in order to avoid cowboy patrols.
Redhorns hunt in small groups of 1d4, these small
bands joining together nightly into herds of 2d6.
Clawed Trample: A redhorn that uses the Charge ac-
tion automatically inflicts 1d8 damage on a target
of man-size or smaller unless the subject has hard
cover within melee range. This damage is in ad-
dition to any done by the charge itself.
Whiskered Devil
These beasts are mutant walruses who have grown
finger-like manipulators along the edges of their flip-
pers and a split trunk to form stubby legs. They can
operate both on land and in the water, and are known
for launching attacks on coastal communities and un-
wary fishing boats. Some coastal waters are so infested
with these creatures that sea travel is all but impossible.
Most have on a feral degree of awareness, but a few
communities of whiskered devils have been uplifted
by Highshine and given a near-human intellect. These
packs often become slavers, taking human thralls to
work island crops or perform labors that clumsy flip-
per-fingers cannot do.
Whiskered devils usually swim in small groups
of 1d4, but a raiding party will number 3d6 of them.
Sea Devil: Whiskered devils can remain submerged
for as long as an hour and have a Move of 20 me
ters when swimming.
